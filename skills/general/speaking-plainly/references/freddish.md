# The Nine Steps of Freddish

Writers for *Mister Rogers' Neighborhood* used these steps to translate an idea into
"Freddish" — language a preschooler could follow without confusion or fear. Each step
rephrases the sentence before it; the same example runs through all nine.

## The Translation Steps

1. **State the idea clearly, in terms a preschooler understands.**
   *"It is dangerous to play in the street."*
2. **Rephrase in a positive manner.**
   *"It is good to play where it is safe."*
3. **Rephrase, redirecting to a trusted authority** — preschoolers can't yet make the
   subtle distinctions the idea requires, so point them to someone who can.
   *"Ask your parents where it is safe to play."*
4. **Remove anything prescriptive, directive, or instructive.**
   *"Your parents will tell you where it is safe to play."*
5. **Remove any element that suggests certainty.**
   *"Your parents can tell you where it is safe to play."*
6. **Remove any element that may not apply to all children.**
   *"Your favorite grown-ups can tell you where it is safe to play."*
7. **Add simple motivation: give a reason to follow the advice.**
   *"Your favorite grown-ups can tell you where it is safe to play. It is good to
   listen to them."*
8. **Repeat step one on the new statement** — state it clearly again.
   *"Your favorite grown-ups can tell you where it is safe to play. It is important
   to try to listen to them."*
9. **Relate the idea to a developmental phase a preschooler can understand.**
   *"Your favorite grown-ups can tell you where it is safe to play. It is important
   to try to listen to them, and listening is an important part of growing."*

Source: Maxwell King, *The Good Neighbor: The Life and Work of Fred Rogers*, excerpted
in The Atlantic (2018), recounting the "Let's Talk About Freddish" manual.
